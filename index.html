/reading

We believe that sharing is killing the open web.

That might seem like a bold statement (and it is), but as the 1% of social media sites continue to dominate our lives, we inch closer to a web that is closed off and controlled by large, billion dollar corporations. No amount of "don't be evil" can protect us.

We've seen these sites go from an open sharing model (when they were smaller, and lesser known) to a model defined by personalized recommendation algorithms, a practice known as filter bubbling. Want to ensure that your 2 year old's picture shows up in her grandmother's feed? You can't.

The plumbing for an open web already exists.

Since the late 90s, authors on the Internet have been able to share their work in an open, standardized way that is simple for computer programs to understand and extract meaning from. We believe that these technologies are mandatory for an open web (yet, the top 1% of social media sites have eliminated them from their feature sets). There are two such formats in common use: RSS and Atom. "Common use" isn't good enough. RSS and Atom should be the plumbing in use whenever possible, on the open web.

There are still sharing focused services that utilize and support RSS and Atom as plumbing.

Many sharing focused services still support this plumbing. We encourage you to utilize those instead of publishing your content into a black hole. In addition, we encourage you to publish 2 OPML files at the root of your website, /reading.xml, and /publishing.xml. Like robots.txt or /purpose, the predictable location is key, so that we can ensure future tools can automatically discover them.

What goes into /reading.xml? /reading.xml is an OPML file that outlines all of the feeds that you consume in your feed reader. Some feeds contain authentication, either because they are customized from a service, or because they are hosting paid content of some sort. These feeds should not be published in /reading.xml.

What goes into /publishing.xml? /publishing.xml is an OPML file outline all of the feeds (Atom/RSS) that you, or your organization are responsible for.

We believe that openness about these things will help us build better and more interesting ways to share and consume shared content in the future, and ultimately protect the open web from closing up.
